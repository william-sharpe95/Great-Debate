Great Debate

Where hot takes become head-to-head battles.

Drop into a daily topic, pick your side, and go toe-to-toe with a real opponent in a private room—then let the judges crown a winner and watch your rank climb.

Overview

People love arguing about the things they care about—but most online spaces turn disagreement into noise: endless threads, bad faith, and no real structure. Great Debate is built on a simple idea: give that energy a daily home where it feels fair, fast, and actually fun.

Each day brings a fresh prompt. You choose a side, get randomly matched with someone on the other side, and argue in a private, timed chat while the rest of the world does the same. When time is up, five AI judges—each with its own persona—reads the full transcript (AI never joins the live debate), picks a winner, and updates your MMR-style ranking. Daily and all-time leaderboards make the competition feel alive, and stored transcripts open the door to sentiment and trend insights across topics over time. For everyday users, it is a social game with bragging rights; for communities, fandoms, or light research and marketing curiosity, it is a window into how opinions cluster and shift. Down the road, debate clubs or classrooms could use the same flow for practice, coaching, and structured communication—without changing what makes the core experience addictive.

To ship it, we would build a daily topic system, side selection, random opponent matching, a private real-time debate chat with clear end rules, and an post-match AI pipeline for judging and optional summaries. We would persist transcripts for later analysis, implement MMR-style rankings, and surface daily and overall leaderboards—plus optional debate-quality feedback users can choose to read after the fact.

Tech stack (student-realistic): a simple frontend in HTML, CSS, and JavaScript; a lightweight backend (for example Flask, or a Firebase/Supabase-style service) for auth, matching, and chat; a small database for users, logs, rankings, and topic history; and API-based LLM calls only after a debate ends—for judging, summaries, trend-style analysis, and optional feedback.
